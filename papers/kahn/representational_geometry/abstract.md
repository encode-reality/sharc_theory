# **Invariant Structure in Predictive Language Models**

## Abstract

This research program advances the thesis that independently evolved symbol systems, when used for large-scale predictive modeling under shared physical constraints, will induce non-trivial structural isomorphisms in their learned representation spaces. Even when primitive symbols, perceptual modalities, and ontological carving differ, embedding geometries trained on sufficiently rich corpora should exhibit partially invariant substructures reflecting conserved physical and thermodynamic constraints.

Large language models project discrete tokens into continuous high-dimensional vector spaces where distributional statistics induce geometric organization. These embedding manifolds encode relational structure, compositionality, causal asymmetries, and optimization dynamics arising from environmental interaction. If two societies optimize predictive compression within the same universe—subject to shared laws such as energy conservation, entropy gradients, scarcity, and temporal asymmetry—then their learned representation spaces should contain homologous submanifolds corresponding to these invariant constraints.

The central hypothesis is that there exists a core invariant region within independently trained embedding spaces that is structurally equivalent up to admissible transformations (e.g., orthogonal or constrained nonlinear mappings). This invariant region reflects survival-relevant predictive abstractions such as resource allocation, state transition structure, causal intervention, and persistence dynamics.

This framework extends empirical results from unsupervised cross-lingual embedding alignment and situates them within a broader computational-functional and information-geometric perspective. Universal translation, under this view, becomes a problem of invariant manifold alignment rather than direct symbol grounding.

Key research directions include:

* Formal characterization of invariant submanifolds induced by shared physical constraints
* Identifiability conditions for unsupervised manifold alignment
* Information-theoretic analysis of minimal sufficient statistics under survival-relevant prediction
* Simulation of cross-species symbolic systems under shared environmental dynamics

This work aims to formalize a theory of representational convergence grounded in predictive compression and physical invariance.
